## The Five Elements

Valid informed consent requires: (1) capacity — the patient can understand, appreciate, reason, and express a choice; (2) disclosure — the clinician shares diagnosis, the proposed intervention, alternatives including no intervention, and material risks and benefits; (3) understanding — the patient demonstrates understanding in their own words, not by passive nodding; (4) voluntariness — the consent is free of coercion, including subtle coercion from family or institutional pressure; (5) authorization — the patient explicitly agrees to the specific intervention.

## What Counts as a "Material Risk"

A risk is material if a reasonable person in the patient's position would want to know it before deciding. The legal threshold is the "reasonable patient" standard, not the "reasonable physician" standard, in most modern jurisdictions including KZ. Common omissions: not mentioning the option of doing nothing; quoting only common side effects and omitting rare-but-serious ones; framing risks only in relative terms.

## Capacity Assessment

Capacity is decision-specific, not global. A patient may have capacity to consent to a blood draw but not to a surgery. Use the four MacCAT-T criteria: understanding, appreciation, reasoning, expressing a choice. Capacity is presumed in adults; the burden of proof is on the clinician who claims it is absent. A patient who refuses recommended treatment does not, by that fact alone, lack capacity.

## When Consent Is Not Required

True medical emergencies in which delay would cause death or serious harm and the patient cannot consent (and no advance directive or surrogate is available). Even then: act in the patient's reasonable best interest, document the emergency, and obtain consent for any further intervention as soon as possible.

## Refusals

A capacitous patient's refusal of any intervention — including life-saving intervention — is legally and ethically valid. The clinician's duty is to ensure the refusal is informed (the patient understands what they are refusing) and to optimize care within the bounds of the refusal. Coercion via family, repeated re-consenting under emotional duress, or "consenting" the patient by surprise are all violations.

## Documentation

Document: what was disclosed, in what language, with what aids (interpreter, diagrams), the patient's questions, the patient's stated understanding, and the explicit authorization or refusal. A signed form alone is not consent — it is evidence that a consent process happened. The process is the consent.
