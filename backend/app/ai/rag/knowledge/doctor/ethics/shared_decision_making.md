## What Shared Decision-Making Is

Shared decision-making (SDM) is a process in which clinicians and patients work together to make decisions, drawing on the best available evidence, the patient's informed preferences, and the patient's values. It is the standard of care for any decision where there is more than one reasonable option (which is most clinical decisions). SDM is not "letting the patient choose" — it is structured deliberation.

## The Three-Talk Model

Team talk: clinician explains that a choice exists and invites the patient to deliberate together. Option talk: clinician describes the options, including doing nothing, with their relative benefits and harms in plain language and absolute numbers (not relative risk). Decision talk: clinician elicits the patient's informed preferences and arrives at a decision the patient can defend in their own words.

## When SDM Is Mandatory

SDM is ethically mandatory whenever (a) more than one reasonable option exists, (b) the options have meaningfully different tradeoffs, and (c) the patient has decision-making capacity. Examples in oncology: watchful waiting vs. immediate biopsy in low-grade presentations; aggressive vs. conservative chemotherapy regimens; participating in a trial vs. standard of care.

## The Numbers Patients Need

Patients understand absolute numbers ("4 in 100 patients"), not relative risk ("40% reduction"). They understand pictographs better than text. They need to hear both "if you do nothing" and "if you choose option X" framed identically. Time horizons (5-year survival, 10-year recurrence) should be explicit.

## Documentation

Document: what options were presented, what numbers were given, what the patient's stated preferences were, and what was decided. SDM that is not documented is legally indistinguishable from paternalism. The chart entry "discussed options, patient agrees" is insufficient.

## Common Errors

(1) Presenting only the option you prefer. (2) Using jargon or relative risk. (3) Forcing the decision in a single visit when the patient needs time. (4) Treating the patient's lack of medical training as inability to deliberate. (5) Documenting only the decision, not the process.
