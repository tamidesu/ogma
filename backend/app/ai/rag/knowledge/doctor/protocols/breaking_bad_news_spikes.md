## Setting (S)

Choose a private, quiet space — not a hallway, not a phone call if avoidable. Sit at the patient's eye level. Silence the pager if possible. If the patient wishes, invite a support person. Allow at least 20 uninterrupted minutes. The physical setting communicates respect before any words are spoken.

## Perception (P)

Before telling, ask. "What have you been told about your condition so far?" or "What are you understanding from the tests we've done?" This calibrates your starting point and surfaces denial, magical thinking, or already-correct understanding. Patients often know more than the chart suggests.

## Invitation (I)

Ask the patient how much they want to know and at what level of detail. "Some patients want every detail; others prefer the headline first and the details later. What works for you?" Respect cultural variation. Some patients explicitly defer to family — that is their choice and must be documented, not overridden.

## Knowledge (K)

Give a short warning shot first ("I have some difficult news"), pause, then deliver the diagnosis in plain language without jargon. Use the disease name. Avoid "tumor" when the diagnosis is "cancer". Pause for 10–20 seconds; do not fill the silence. Do not give prognosis or treatment plan in the same breath as the diagnosis — let the news land first.

## Empathy (E)

Name the emotion you observe. "I can see this is devastating." Do not rush to reassure ("but the survival rate is high…") — premature reassurance cuts off grief and signals the doctor's discomfort, not the patient's. The single most important moment is the silent acknowledgement that this news has changed their life.

## Strategy and Summary (S)

Only after the patient signals readiness do you move to plan: tests, specialists, timing, what happens next. End by checking comprehension — ask the patient to summarize what they've understood, not yes/no questions. Schedule a follow-up within 1–7 days; the second conversation is when most planning actually happens.

## Common Errors

(1) Delivering the diagnosis with the door open or while standing. (2) Burying the diagnosis in a stream of statistics ("the 5-year survival is 95%, oh and you have lymphoma"). (3) Using euphemisms that obscure the truth. (4) Filling silence with reassurance. (5) Discharging the patient the same day with no follow-up scheduled. (6) Telling family members before the patient.
