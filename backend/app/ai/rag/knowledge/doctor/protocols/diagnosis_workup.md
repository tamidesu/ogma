## Initial Evaluation of Persistent Lymphadenopathy

A palpable lymph node persisting beyond 2–4 weeks in an adult, especially when accompanied by constitutional symptoms (B symptoms: unexplained weight loss >10% over 6 months, drenching night sweats, persistent fever >38°C), warrants tissue diagnosis. Watchful waiting beyond 4 weeks in this presentation is below standard of care and may delay treatment of a treatable malignancy.

## History and Physical

A focused history must capture: duration, growth pattern, B symptoms, recent infections, exposures (TB, HIV risk factors), family history of malignancy (especially first-degree relatives with lymphoma, breast, or any cancer before age 50), and medication history. The physical exam should map all node stations (cervical, supraclavicular, axillary, inguinal), assess for splenomegaly, and document size in centimeters with consistency (rubbery vs. firm vs. matted).

## First-Line Investigations

CBC with differential, peripheral smear, ESR, LDH, comprehensive metabolic panel, HIV serology, hepatitis B/C screening. Imaging: chest X-ray for mediastinal disease screening; if abnormal or if the node is supraclavicular, proceed to contrast-enhanced CT of neck/chest/abdomen/pelvis. PET-CT is reserved for staging after diagnosis.

## Tissue Diagnosis

Excisional lymph node biopsy is the gold standard for suspected lymphoma. Fine-needle aspiration is insufficient because architecture matters for classification. Refer to surgery for excisional biopsy within 1–2 weeks if lymphoma is on the differential. Core needle biopsy is acceptable only if surgical risk is high; pathologist must be informed lymphoma is suspected so adequate immunohistochemistry is performed.

## Specialist Referral Threshold

Refer to hematology/oncology when: tissue diagnosis confirms lymphoma; or when clinical suspicion is high but biopsy is delayed or non-diagnostic. Do not delay referral while waiting for "more workup" — the specialist can drive parallel investigations. Telephone referral is acceptable for urgent cases.

## Common Errors

(1) Prescribing empiric antibiotics without a clear infectious source — masks symptoms without treating the underlying disease. (2) Watchful waiting longer than 4 weeks for a node that is supraclavicular, >2cm, hard, or accompanied by B symptoms. (3) Ordering PET-CT before tissue diagnosis — wastes time and money, doesn't replace pathology. (4) Reassuring the patient prematurely before pathology returns.
